Here’s a Putnam problem that I’m thinking about. It is Problem A6 of 1985.

If \(p(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + \cdots + a_m x^m\) is a polynomial with real coefficients \(a_i\), then set \[\Gamma(p(x)) = a_0^2 + \cdots + a_m^2.\] Let \(f(x) = 3x^2 + 7x + 2\). Find, with proof, a polynomial \(g(x)\) with real coefficients such that i) \(g(0) = 1\), and ii) \(\Gamma(f(x)^n) = \Gamma(g(x)^n)\) for every integer \(n\).

Why am I thinking about a Putnam problem? Well-known mathematicians still publish about them. Also Richard Feynman and David Mumford were Putnam fellows. I’ve noticed during my 2.5 years in Bonn the difference between moving too slowly during a math study session and being dynamic and quick enough. I’ve brute forced a lot of study sessions to my own detriment. As I’ve recently been more successful in my studies, being dynamic with my study sessions has been a key change. But I still get stubborn and fall into boring loops and get pedantic - moving too slow. I think Putnam problems will give me a good feel for the mental agility needed for research problems.

April 12, 2026

Smooth immersions are maps whose differentials are injective everywhere. Smooth embeddings are injective smooth immersions that are also homeomorphisms onto their images. Smooth immersions and embeddings are key ingredients in the theory of submanifolds.

The rank of a linear map \(L: V \to W\) is the dimension of its range: \(\dim L(V)\). Given two linear maps \(L_1, L_2\), we can find two change of basis maps \(B_1, B_2\) such that \(L_1 = B_1 \circ L_2 \circ B_2\) if and only if the rank of \(L_1\) and \(L_2\) is the same.

The rank of a smooth map between manifolds \(F: M \to N\) at a point \(p\) is the rank of the differential of that smooth map at the point \(p\).

A linear map is injective if and only if its kernel is the \(\{0\}\) space. Equivalently, a linear map \(L: V \to W\) is injective if and only if \(\dim L(V) = \dim V\), because then no dimensions are left for the kernel.

A smooth map has constant rank if its rank at every point on the manifold is the same. A smooth map \(F\) of constant rank also has full rank if the rank of its differential at all points is as large as possible: either equal to the dimension of its domain or codomain. By logic, smooth immersions are smooth maps of constant rank and of full rank whose rank is equal to the dimension of the tangent space of the domain manifold.

The mapping \(\iota: SU(2) \to \mathbb C^{2 \times 2} \cong \mathbb R^8\) is smooth provided that for every coordinate system \(\xi\) in \(SU(2)\) and \(\eta\) in \(\mathbb C^{2 \times 2}\), the coordinate expression \(\eta \circ \iota \circ \xi^{-1}\) is Euclidean smooth and defined on an open set of \(\mathbb R^3\).

We have \(C^{\infty}(M) = \{f: M \to \mathbb R: f \text{ smooth in the sense of manifold theory} \}\).

Let \(M\) be a topological space and let \(N \subset M\). The subspace topology on \(N\) is the collection: \(\{ N \cap U: U \text{ is open in } M\}\).

Let \(E\) be a module over a commutative ring \(R\). Let \(g: E \times E \to R\) be a map. If \(g\) is bilinear, we call \(g\) a symmetric form if \(g(x,y) = g(y,x)\) for all \(x,y \in E\). If \(R\) has an automorphism of order 2 written \(a \mapsto \overline{a}\), we say \(g\) is a Hermitian form if it is linear in its first variable, antilinear in its second, and \(g(x,y) = \overline{g(y,x)}\). In either case, we call \(g\) a scalar product.

What is a left-invariant vector field on \(SU(2)\)? Why does the tangent space \(T_A SU(2) = A \cdot \mathfrak{su}(2)\) for \(A \in SU(2)\)?

Let \(\mathfrak{X}(M)\) denote the set of all smooth vector fields on \(M\). Note that \(C^\infty(M)\) is a commutative ring, with addition given by pointwise addition of functions, and multiplication given by pointwise multiplication of functions. It is not a field because a smooth function which takes the value \(0\) somewhere is not invertible in the sense of multiplication. Then \(\mathfrak{X}(M)\) is a \(C^\infty(M)\)-module. To verify this, think about why \(\mathfrak{X}(M)\) is a group under pointwise addition. Then think about how a smooth function \(f \in C^{\infty}(M)\) acts on a vector field, and think about why \((f + g)V = fV + gV\) follows from pointwise considerations. This module has a basis given by the smooth sections \((\partial_i)_{i = 1}^n\). So the coefficients in a linear combination would be smooth functions on \(M\).

A derivation on \(C^{\infty}(M)\) is a function \(D: C^{\infty}(M) \to C^{\infty}(M)\) that is \(\mathbb R\)-linear and Leibnizian. If \(V \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\) is a vector field, then the map \(\Psi: C^{\infty}(M) \ni f \mapsto Vf \in C^{\infty}(M)\) is a derivation. Let’s check that \(\Psi\) is Leibnizian. Let \(f,g \in C^{\infty}(M)\). Fix \(p \in M\). Then \(V_p \in T_p M\). By definition of tangent vector at \(p\), we note that \(\Psi(fg)(p) = V_p(fg) = f(p) V_p(g) + V_p(f) g(p) = f(p) \Psi(g)(p) + \Psi(f)(p) g(p),\) so the Leibnizian property holds pointwise.

Every derivation comes from a vector field. Fix a derivation \(D: C^{\infty}(M) \to C^{\infty}(M)\). Notice that when \(p\) is a point on the manifold \(M\), then \(C^{\infty}(M) \ni f \mapsto (Df)(p) \in \mathbb R\) if a rule assigning functions to real numbers. Recall that a vector field \(V \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\), at each point on the manifold, assigns functions to real numbers. So we suppose that if \(V\) does exist, then it should satisfy \(V_p f = (Df)(p)\). The class of smooth functions are rich enough to determine \(V_p\) based on this requirement.

How do we view \(Vf\) as a smooth function when \(V\) is a vector field and when \(f\) is a smooth function?

How can we consider vector fields as derivations \(C^{\infty}(M) \to C^{\infty}(M)\)?
